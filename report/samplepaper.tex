\begin{abstract}
We present an unsupervised analytics pipeline that discovers latent tactical identities --- Tactical Fingerprints --- from football event data and player passing networks. Applying the pipeline to the Wyscout public corpus (3.25 million events, 1,941 matches, 154 teams, seven competitions), we extract 30 features per team-season combining 21 event-derived style metrics and 9 graph-theoretic passing-network properties. Dimensionality reduction via PCA followed by agglomerative hierarchical clustering with Ward linkage produces five statistically distinct archetypes: Elite Possession, Balanced Mid-Table, Structured Attack, Defensive Direct, and Direct Long Ball. Kruskal--Wallis tests confirm that the fingerprints are strongly associated with match outcomes (win rate: $H = 36.2$, $p = 2.6 \times 10^{-7}$; points per match: $H = 37.7$, $p = 1.3 \times 10^{-7}$; goal difference: $H = 42.7$, $p = 1.2 \times 10^{-8}$). The pipeline is fully reproducible via a Google Colab notebook and a CLI runner backed by a modular Python package.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Modern football clubs generate millions of match events per season, yet most tactical conversations still rely on anecdote and the eye test. Summary statistics such as possession percentage or pass completion rate aggregate away the texture of play and cannot reliably answer whether an upcoming opponent favours direct transitions, rotational build-up, or high-press recovery.

This paper presents Tactical Fingerprints, an end-to-end pipeline that answers those questions at scale. The pipeline is applied to the Wyscout public dataset covering five domestic leagues and two international tournaments (1,941 matches, 3.25 million events, 154 team-season profiles). From this corpus we derive 30 features per team — 21 event-derived style metrics and 9 passing-network topology metrics — and cluster the resulting profiles into five fingerprints using agglomerative hierarchical clustering. Kruskal–Wallis tests confirm that the five archetypes differ significantly on win rate, points per match, and goal difference.

The contributions of this work are: (i) a joint event-plus-network feature space validated against match outcomes; (ii) a five-archetype taxonomy of European professional football playing styles; and (iii) a reproducible pipeline any club analytics department can adapt to its own data.

\section{Related Work}

Early football analytics focused on summary statistics from match reports \cite{reep1968}. The event-data era enabled more granular analyses: Lago-Pe\~nas et al.\ showed possession metrics correlate with outcomes \cite{lago2010}, while passing network analysis introduced by Duch et al.\ modelled players as nodes and passes as weighted directed edges \cite{duch2010}. Cintia et al.\ extended this to style characterisation, showing network topology captures team organisation differences \cite{cintia2015}. Clustering has been applied to football formations \cite{bialkowski2014}, shot quality \cite{brooks2016}, and player profiles \cite{pappalardo2019}. Our contribution is a joint clustering of event and network features at the team-season level, validated with non-parametric statistics.

\section{Data}

\subsection{Wyscout Public Dataset}

We use the Wyscout public dataset \cite{pappalardo2019}, available at figshare collection 4415000/5, covering the 2017/18 seasons of five top European leagues and UEFA Euro 2016 and the 2018 FIFA World Cup. After loading and parsing, the corpus comprises 3,251,294 events from 1,941 matches played by 142 distinct clubs, yielding 154 team-season entries treated as independent observations.

Each event record contains \texttt{matchId}, \texttt{teamId}, \texttt{playerId}, \texttt{eventId}, \texttt{subEventId}, \texttt{matchPeriod}, \texttt{eventSec}, and a \texttt{positions} array of normalised $x$--$y$ coordinates ($x \in [0, 100]$ towards the opponent goal). Passes are identified by \texttt{eventId = 8} with sub-event codes for crosses (80), simple passes (85), smart passes (86), and long balls (84). Accurate events carry tag 1801; counter-attacks are flagged by tag 1901.

\subsection{Preprocessing}

Events are enriched with competition and outcome labels from the matches file (\texttt{winner = 0} denotes a draw). Spatial zones are assigned along the $x$-axis: $x < 33.3$ (defensive third), $x \in [33.3, 66.6]$ (midfield third), $x > 66.6$ (attacking third). Boolean flags are added for accurate events and counter-attack events. After cleaning, 3,241,068 events remain for analysis.

\section{Feature Engineering}

We construct 30 features per (\texttt{matchId}, \texttt{teamId}) observation, subsequently averaged to one season-level profile per team-competition entry.

\subsection{Event Features (21)}

\textbf{Volume} features count total events, passes, shots, and duels. \textbf{Efficiency} features quantify pass accuracy, shot accuracy, and duel win rate (tag 703) as ratios. \textbf{Spatial} features record mean and standard deviation of event positions in both dimensions. \textbf{Zone} features decompose passing volume into defensive, midfield, and attacking thirds. \textbf{Style} features capture proportions of crosses, simple passes, smart passes, lofted passes, long balls, counter-attacks, and dangerous attacks.

\subsection{Passing Network Features (9)}

For each (\texttt{matchId}, \texttt{teamId}) pair we build a directed weighted graph whose nodes are players and whose edges encode passing sequences. Because Wyscout does not record pass receivers directly, we use consecutive accurate pass events (sorted by \texttt{matchPeriod} and \texttt{eventSec}) as a proxy for passer--receiver relationships; only accurate passes (tag 1801) are included. From each graph we extract nine metrics: graph density, local clustering coefficient, weighted clustering coefficient, degree centralisation, top-player share of passing volume, average shortest path length, diameter, reciprocity, and node count.

\subsection{Team-Season Profiles}

Per-match features are aggregated to season level by computing the mean across all matches a team played in a given competition, producing 154 team-season vectors as input to clustering.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Dimensionality Reduction}

Each 30-dimensional feature vector is standardised to zero mean and unit variance. PCA with 10 components is applied to the standardised matrix. For two-dimensional visualisation only, UMAP is applied to the same standardised matrix; UMAP plays no role in the clustering itself.

\subsection{Clustering}

Agglomerative hierarchical clustering with Ward linkage is applied to the PCA-10 representation. The number of clusters $k$ is selected by evaluating the silhouette coefficient and Davies--Bouldin index for $k = 2$ through $k = 8$.

The silhouette coefficient peaks at $k = 2$ ($s = 0.284$) and declines monotonically; at $k = 5$ it yields $s = 0.165$. The Davies--Bouldin index shows a local improvement at $k = 5$, providing partial corroboration for a finer partition.

We select $k = 5$ for three domain-driven reasons. First, $k = 2$ produces only a coarse binary split; $k = 5$ recovers five qualitatively distinct profiles recognisable to coaches. Second, $k = 2$ yields clusters of 12 and 142 teams, which is analytically unbalanced; $k = 5$ yields clusters of 8--50 teams. Third, Kruskal--Wallis tests (Section~\ref{sec:validation}) confirm the five clusters differ significantly on all outcome measures. The modest absolute silhouette values reflect that football playing styles form a continuum rather than tight, well-separated clusters.

\subsection{Statistical Validation}
\label{sec:validation}

The Kruskal--Wallis $H$-test is applied to three outcome variables --- win rate, points per match, and goal difference --- to assess whether fingerprints are associated with performance. We use $\alpha = 0.05$ as the significance threshold.

\subsubsection{FP0 -- Balanced Mid-Table ($n = 50$).}
The largest cluster captures typical mid-table sides with average pass accuracy (0.828), moderate network density (0.610), and balanced zone distribution. Win rate (0.347) and goal difference ($-0.141$) confirm near-average performance.

\subsubsection{FP1 -- Elite Possession ($n = 12$).}
The smallest and most distinctive cluster captures elite possession-based teams: the highest pass accuracy (0.882), the highest network density (0.681), the lowest long-ball ratio (0.011), and the best performance on all three outcome metrics (win rate 0.582, 1.930 points per match, $+0.955$ goal difference). The low centralization score (0.491) reflects that passing is distributed across many players rather than routed through a single hub --- a hallmark of fluid possession football. Teams such as Real Madrid, Barcelona, and Bayern Munich are expected members of this cluster.

\subsubsection{FP2 -- Direct Long Ball ($n = 8$).}
This small cluster is defined by the highest long-ball ratio (0.054) and cross ratio (0.046) and the lowest pass accuracy (0.776), indicating teams that play direct, often aerial football. The highest centralization score (0.582) suggests routing through a target forward or wide player. Performance is poor (win rate 0.278, $-0.385$ goal difference), consistent with research showing that direct-play styles are less effective at the top level. The cluster primarily draws from tournament teams, which partly accounts for its small size.

\subsubsection{FP3 -- Defensive Direct ($n = 49$).}
The second-largest cluster groups teams that play direct, low-accuracy football (0.788) with a high long-ball ratio (0.043) but achieve less attacking output than FP2 (lower cross ratio 0.044). The worst goal difference of all five clusters ($-0.467$) and a win rate of 0.279 characterise these as tactically limited sides, typically lower-league finishers and weaker tournament participants.


\subsubsection{FP4 -- Structured Attack ($n = 30$).}
This mid-to-upper cluster describes organised, competent sides that do not reach the passing fluency of Elite Possession teams but achieve above-average outcomes (win rate 0.467, 1.626 points per match, $+0.304$ goal difference). Pass accuracy (0.854) and network density (0.655) are second only to FP1. The relatively low long-ball ratio (0.020) and cross ratio (0.033) point to patient build-up play balanced with direct attacking.

\subsection{Statistical Validation}

Kruskal--Wallis tests reject the null hypothesis of identical outcome distributions across all five fingerprints at $p < 0.001$ on every metric (Table~\ref{tab:kruskal}). The strongest separation is on goal difference ($H = 42.66$): the gap between FP1 ($+0.955$) and FP3 ($-0.467$) represents nearly 1.4 goals per match.

\subsection{Cross-Competition Analysis}

Fingerprint membership is not uniformly distributed across competitions. The Elite Possession cluster (FP1) is almost exclusively composed of domestic league champions and second-place finishers in the five top leagues --- clubs that qualified for the Champions League in subsequent seasons --- confirming that the archetype correctly identifies elite-level play. Tournament teams (Euro 2016 and World Cup 2018) are disproportionately represented in FP2 and FP3, likely reflecting the smaller match samples available for these teams (Section~7.1).

Within domestic leagues, tactical style varies systematically across competitions. Pass accuracy ranges from approximately 0.81 in Ligue 1 to 0.85 in La Liga. Smart-pass ratio and attacking-third passing proportion are highest in the Premier League and La Liga, consistent with these leagues' reputations for high-tempo, technical football. Long-ball ratio is highest in the Bundesliga and among English sides, reflecting those leagues' physical playing traditions.

\subsection{Fingerprint Lookup and Client Delivery}

The five Tactical Fingerprints are operationalised into three concrete deliverables for the football analytics department.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Archetype card.} Each fingerprint is summarised in a one-page radar chart showing its normalised values across the eight most discriminative features (pass accuracy, long-ball ratio, cross ratio, smart-pass ratio, network density, centralisation, attacking-third pass proportion, counter-attack rate), together with its win rate, points per match, and goal difference. The card is designed to be printed and shared in a pre-match briefing without requiring any statistical knowledge from the coaching staff.

    \item \textbf{Team lookup.} Any team in the dataset is assigned its fingerprint label and ranked within that fingerprint on each feature. A club's analyst can query ``where does Opponent X sit within FP2, and which of their features are most extreme?'' in a single table view, enabling fast opponent scouting preparation.

    \item \textbf{Gap analysis for recruitment.} A club wishing to shift from FP3 (Defensive Direct) toward FP4 (Structured Attack) can generate a gap table showing the mean feature difference between the two fingerprints on each of the 30 dimensions. Recruitment candidates are then filtered by those gap features --- prioritising pass accuracy, network density, and attacking-third passing --- rather than by traditional metrics alone. This transforms the fingerprint taxonomy from a descriptive tool into a practical recruitment filter.
\end{enumerate}
\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Limitations}

The dataset covers only two seasons; whether the five archetypes persist across a broader sample is an open question. The passer--receiver approximation from consecutive events introduces noise when sequences are interrupted by events belonging to the opposing team. Most critically, domestic league teams have approximately 38 match observations while tournament teams have only 3--7, causing season-level averages to carry considerably higher variance for the latter group. Future work should either restrict analysis to domestic leagues or apply regularisation to stabilise sparse-team profiles.

The silhouette coefficient favours $k = 2$ over $k = 5$. As argued in Section~5.2, $k = 5$ is selected on domain and analytical grounds, but the modest silhouette values confirm that playing style is a continuum and any discrete partitioning involves interpretation.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

The Tactical Fingerprint framework has three practical applications for football practitioners. First, it provides a data-driven taxonomy of playing styles that coaches can use to contextualise their own team and benchmark against peers. A newly promoted side finding itself in FP3 (Defensive Direct) can identify the feature gaps separating it from FP4 (Structured Attack) and target specific improvements --- for instance, increasing passing accuracy in the midfield third or reducing centralisation to develop more distributed build-up play.

Second, the fingerprints support recruitment targeting. A club seeking to shift from FP3 to FP4 can filter transfer candidates by the feature profiles most diagnostic of that transition (pass accuracy, network density, attacking third pass proportion) rather than searching only on traditional metrics.

Third, the framework enables opponent scouting: knowing that an upcoming opponent belongs to FP2 (Direct Long Ball) with high centralisation suggests deploying a compact defensive shape targeting the key distribution hub rather than defending wide channels.

\section{Conclusion}

We have presented a principled, reproducible pipeline for discovering Tactical Fingerprints from event data and passing networks. Applied to the Wyscout corpus, the pipeline identifies five statistically validated playing-style archetypes that differ substantially in pass accuracy, network topology, zone preferences, and match outcomes. Elite Possession teams win at nearly twice the rate of Direct Long Ball teams, and the goal difference gap between the top and bottom archetypes exceeds one goal per match. The joint feature space — combining 21 event features with 9 graph-theoretic network metrics — proves more discriminative than either family alone. Future extensions could incorporate tracking data, add pressure metrics, and study style evolution longitudinally.
